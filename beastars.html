Beastars

In a world of anthropomorphic animals, the eating of other animals is strictly taboo — but instinct and society's rules are in permanent, unresolved tension. Grey wolf Legoshi is large, terrifying-looking, and gently non-violent. When a classmate is devoured in the school theatre, the question of who — and why — exposes the fault lines running through everyone.

Suggested rating: TV-MA Suggested age: 17+ Content note: predation themes, violence

Core Themes

  • Instinct vs. choice — being born with urges vs. who you decide to be
  • Systemic discrimination hidden beneath enforced civility
  • Love across a divide society insists cannot be crossed

Story overview

An animal world using biology as social commentary.

Act 1: The Devouring

The murder of alpaca student Tem destabilises the Cherryton High drama club. Legoshi, a wolf who has suppressed his predatory instincts his entire life, becomes a suspect — and simultaneously falls for dwarf rabbit Haru, putting both his self-control and his understanding of himself under extreme pressure.

Act 2: The Black Market

Beneath the school's herbivore-carnivore peace is a criminal underworld where meat is sold illegally. Legoshi's investigation takes him into this world — and toward a confrontation with the Beastar (the ideal of what an animal can be) that school president Louis is working furiously to become.

Act 3: Louis and the Lions

Louis, a red deer being groomed for greatness, makes choices that place him in the lion mafia — and reveal the performances of power that underlie the entire social fabric. His arc is about what it costs a prey animal to survive in a world that respects only predators.

Season 2: Melon

The second season introduces Melon — a hybrid carnivore/herbivore who represents the failure of both worlds' ideals — and escalates into a conflict about what society does with those it cannot categorise. Darker and more ambitious than season one.

Fun facts

Behind the CG and the social allegory.

The anime uses 3DCG animation from Studio Orange.

Studio Orange (Land of the Lustrous) applied their signature photorealistic CG style to Beastars — the fur rendering was widely praised as a technical achievement.

The manga won the Manga Taishou Award in 2018.

Paru Itagaki's work was commercially and critically successful before the anime — she is the daughter of Baki creator Keisuke Itagaki, and the contrast in their styles is frequently discussed.

The series is widely read as a racism and class anxiety allegory.

The herbivore/carnivore divide maps onto multiple real-world discriminations — structural disadvantage, profiling, and the violence of civility enforced on those whose existence is considered inherently threatening.
