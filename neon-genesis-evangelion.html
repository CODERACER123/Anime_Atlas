Neon Genesis Evangelion

In 2015, mysterious entities called Angels attack humanity. 14-year-old Shinji Ikari is summoned by his absent father to pilot the biomechanical mecha Eva Unit-01 — the only weapon that can fight back. But the real war is the one inside Shinji's own mind.

Suggested rating: TV-MA Suggested age: 17+ Content note: depression, existential themes

Story overview

A mecha anime that became something else entirely.

Act 1: Shinji Pilots Unit-01

Shinji reluctantly pilots the Eva and begins defeating Angels alongside pilots Rei Ayanami and Asuka Langley Soryu. The action sequences are extraordinary — but between fights, the series probes each character's psychological wounds with unusual depth.

Act 2: Cracks in NERV

The organisation NERV, run by Shinji's cold father Gendo, has a secret purpose — one hidden beneath the fight against Angels. The Seele committee pulls strings none of the pilots understand. Trust dissolves.

Episodes 25–26: The Instrumentality

The original TV ending replaces external events with internal introspection — a deeply personal, fractured exploration of each character's psychology. One of anime's most divisive and influential finales.

End of Evangelion (Film)

The theatrical alternative ending shows what was happening externally during the final two episodes — apocalyptic, overwhelming, and emotionally devastating. Together with the series, it forms one complete work.

Fun facts

The show that changed anime forever.

Creator Hideaki Anno was battling depression during production.

Anno's psychological state during NGE's creation is widely discussed and acknowledged — the series is partly autobiographical in its exploration of depression and self-hatred.

The production ran out of budget in the final episodes.

The famously abstract TV finale was partly a result of budget constraints — but Anno also turned necessity into artistic statement, creating something unlike any action anime.

The Rebuild of Evangelion tetralogy reimagines the story across four films.

Released from 2007 to 2021, the Rebuild films offer a different — and more resolved — take on the same narrative, with dramatically different outcomes.
