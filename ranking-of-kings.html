Ranking of Kings

Prince Bojji is deaf, mute, and too weak to lift a sword. The world ranks kings by power — and by every measure, he is last. His only friend is Kage, a shadow creature who teaches him to communicate. Their journey to become king, and what that word actually means, is one of anime's most devastating emotional experiences.

Suggested rating: TV-PG Suggested age: 12+

Core Themes

  • Strength defined by something other than physical power
  • Every "villain" has a history that made them what they are
  • Goodness as a radical act in a world built on conquest

Story overview

A fairy-tale aesthetic concealing a story of extraordinary depth.

Act 1: Bojji and Kage

The storybook visual style is immediately disarming — but Bojji's isolation, the cruelty of those who mock him, and Kage's own history of loss are established with genuine emotional weight. Their friendship is the series' emotional anchor.

Act 2: The Kingdom's Secrets

Bojji's stepmother Hiling and half-brother Daida reveal their full complexity — Hiling's fierce maternal love, Daida's tragic manipulation by the mirror demon Miranjo. No character in this series is simply a villain.

Act 3: The Tournament

The ranking system that defines the series' world is revealed to be both literal and deeply flawed — the things it measures are not the things that matter. Bojji's approach to every challenge redefines what the ranking should mean.

Finale: What Makes a King

Every character's arc converges in a finale that rejects easy resolutions — each character's goodness or cruelty is traced back to their wounds, and the series' answer to "what makes a king" is genuinely surprising and earned.

Fun facts

The Wit Studio adaptation that became a phenomenon.

The art style deceived many viewers initially.

The simple, rounded character designs look like a children's fairy tale — and completely misrepresent the narrative's emotional sophistication. Many viewers were blindsided by how affecting it becomes.

Bojji's sign language was developed in consultation with experts.

The production worked to make Bojji and Kage's communication system internally consistent and emotionally expressive — it's not just hand-waving, it has actual vocabulary.

Every major antagonist receives a sympathetic backstory.

Unlike most fantasy series, Ranking of Kings commits fully to showing why every "villain" became what they are — and allows viewers to grieve for them, not just defeat them.

Key characters

  • Bojji — the deaf prince who redefines strength
  • Kage — last of the shadow clan, Bojji's best friend
  • Daida — Bojji's half-brother, a puppet who reclaims himself
  • Miranjo — the mirror demon, the saddest villain in the series
