Paranoia Agent

A boy on golden roller blades and wielding a bent golden bat begins attacking seemingly unrelated people across Tokyo. Detectives Ikari and Maniwa investigate — but "Lil' Slugger" may be more than a criminal. He may be everything his victims secretly wished for.

Core Themes

  • Social pressure and the desire to escape responsibility
  • Mass hysteria and how rumour shapes reality
  • The manufactured refuge of fiction and delusion

Story overview

Thirteen episodes. Thirteen different people who needed him.

Episodes 1–5: The Victims

Each early episode focuses on a new character under crushing social or personal pressure — and each is attacked. The victims seem to have nothing in common, yet each attack comes at the perfect moment to rescue them from something worse.

Episodes 6–8: Strange Detours

Mid-series, the show deliberately abandons its central mystery for standalone episodes — one following three characters planning to die, one set entirely within a fabricated anime world. Satoshi Kon tests how far audiences will follow him.

Episodes 9–11: Spreading Paranoia

The Lil' Slugger phenomenon becomes a cultural virus — children emulate him, false confessions pour in, the media amplifies the panic. The detective investigation fractures under the weight of what they're discovering.

Episodes 12–13: The Source

The origin of Lil' Slugger is revealed — one that implicates a creation of the imagination itself. The finale is not fully explainable. That is very much the point.

Fun facts

Satoshi Kon's only TV series.

Satoshi Kon created this as his only original TV anime.

The director of Perfect Blue, Millennium Actress, and Paprika produced Paranoia Agent as ideas that couldn't fit in his films — giving them space to breathe across 13 episodes.

The opening sequence is one of anime's most iconic.

Characters laughing in impossible, dangerous, surreal situations while a cheerful pop song plays — it sets the show's unsettling tone before a single plot point is established.

Every episode has a different visual style and tone.

Kon and director Masashi Ando deliberately shifted the series' look and approach episode by episode — making it feel both unified and constantly surprising.
