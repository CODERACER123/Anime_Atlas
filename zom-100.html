Zom 100: Bucket List of the Dead

Akira Tendo spent three years in a soul-destroying job — unpaid overtime, toxic management, zero personal time. Then zombies take over Japan and he experiences pure joy for the first time in years: he doesn't have to go to work. He writes a bucket list of 100 things to do before he becomes a zombie. The apocalypse is the best thing that ever happened to him.

Suggested rating: TV-14 Suggested age: 14+ Content note: zombie violence, workplace abuse themes

Core Themes

  • Japan's karoshi (overwork) culture as a slow death before the zombies
  • Freedom reclaimed — the apocalypse as permission to live
  • Other survivors whose "surviving" definitions vary wildly

Story overview

The zombie apocalypse as liberation.

Act 1: The List Begins

The first episode establishes Akira's pre-zombie life — genuinely harrowing corporate abuse — before the zombies arrive. His joy is not a joke; it is earned. The bucket list items range from silly to meaningful, and the series commits to all of them.

Act 2: Kencho and Shizuka

Akira meets old friend Kencho and survival-focused Shizuka, whose entirely different approach to the apocalypse — treating it as a crisis to managed efficiently rather than a windfall of freedom — creates productive friction.

Act 3: Survivor Communities

The group encounters different communities of survivors — each with their own philosophy of how to live in the new world. Some have rebuilt old hierarchies. Some have created new ones. None are as simple as they appear.

Ongoing: The Nature of Living

The bucket list slowly transforms from escapism to genuine self-exploration — each item Akira completes tells him something about who he was before, and who he wants to be now that nobody is forcing him to be anything.

Fun facts

Behind the colourful apocalypse and the workplace critique.

The series is an explicit critique of Japan's overwork culture.

The manga by Haro Aso and Kotaro Takata opens with Akira's working conditions described in the language of horror — deliberately framing the corporate world as the real zombie situation.

BUG FILMS produced the anime in 2023 with distinctive vivid colour.

The anime's saturated, pop-art visual palette was a deliberate choice — the contrast between bright visuals and zombie carnage reinforces the series' life-affirmation tone.

Shizuka's arc deconstructs the "survival-first" mentality.

Her character is designed to challenge Akira's approach — not because she's wrong about survival, but because the series wants to examine what surviving without living actually means.
