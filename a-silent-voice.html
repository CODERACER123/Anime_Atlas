A Silent Voice

As a child, Shoya Ishida bullied Shoko Nishimiya — a deaf girl — until she transferred schools. Years later, haunted by guilt and a social outcast himself, he seeks her out not for absolution, but because she deserves an apology he may not deserve to give.

Suggested rating: PG-13 Suggested age: 13+ Content note: bullying, suicidal ideation

Core Themes

  • Genuine redemption vs. self-serving guilt
  • The systemic failure of bystanders
  • Loving yourself enough to allow others to love you

Story overview

An apology years in the making — and everything that has to happen first.

Act 1: The Bullying

Elementary school. Shoko transfers in — deaf, armed with a notebook, trying her hardest. Shoya leads the class in making her life miserable, breaking seven hearing aids. When it goes too far, the class turns on Shoya to avoid blame.

Act 2: Carrying the Weight

Years later, Shoya is in high school and completely isolated — he has mentally X'd out every face around him. He has been learning sign language and tracks Shoko down, not sure what he even wants to say.

Act 3: Rebuilding

The two rebuild a careful, tentative friendship, joined by a group of characters each carrying their own forms of guilt, loneliness, and unsaid truths. Nothing is simple or quickly resolved.

Finale: The Bridge

A crisis forces everyone to face what they've been avoiding. The final act is devastating and then, slowly, genuinely hopeful — built on real change rather than easy forgiveness.

Fun facts

Behind the film and its impact.

Directed by Naoko Yamada at Kyoto Animation.

Yamada's direction focuses intimately on hands, feet, and non-verbal communication — a deliberate visual language for a story about connection without words.

The film is widely praised by the deaf community.

The depiction of Shoko's experience — including imperfect sign language and the social complications of deafness — was reviewed as unusually nuanced and respectful.

The X's over faces are a carefully designed visual metaphor.

Shoya sees everyone's face obscured by an X — his inability to engage with others made literal. When they fade, it signals real psychological change.
