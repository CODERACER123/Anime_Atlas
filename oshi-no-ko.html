Idol • Mystery • Dark Industry

Oshi no Ko

A doctor who idolised pop star Ai Hoshino is reincarnated as her newborn son — with all his memories intact. He watches his mother's entertainment career from inside it, and when she is murdered, he vows to uncover who did it and destroy them.

Suggested rating: TV-MA Suggested age: 17+ Content note: murder, entertainment industry exploitation

Story overview

Born into the industry — to destroy it from inside.

Prologue: The Doctor and the Star

90-minute first episode. Doctor Gorou is obsessed with idol Ai Hoshino — then becomes her OB/GYN, discovers her secret pregnancy, and is murdered before the birth. He wakes as her son, Aqua, with his memories. His twin sister Ruby is the reincarnation of his dying patient.

Act 2: Childhood in the Industry

Aqua grows up watching the idol industry destroy performers from inside. When Ai is murdered by an obsessive fan connected to someone who knew her secret, Aqua discovers the trail leads to someone tied to his own father's identity.

Act 3: The Reality Show Arc

Aqua, Ruby, and their acquaintances participates in a reality dating show — an arc that becomes a devastating examination of how staged "authenticity" works in entertainment, and how easily it can destroy real people.

Ongoing: The Stage Play and Beyond

A 2.5D stage adaptation of Ai's life draws all the key players together. The investigation nears its target as Aqua gets closer to the truth — and closer to what completing his revenge will cost him.

Fun facts

Behind the most shocking first episode in recent memory.

The 90-minute premiere broke streaming records.

HIDIVE and other platforms reported record view counts for the extended first episode — the shocking ending of the prologue spread virally immediately after release.

Written by Aka Akasaka (Kaguya-sama) and art by Mengo Yokoyari (Scum's Wish).

The collaboration combines Akasaka's twisty, comedic romance plotting with Yokoyari's darker emotional sensibility — resulting in a manga unlike either of their solo works.

The series is a searing critique of Japan's idol culture.

The "lie as performance" theme directly engages with the constructed personas, parasocial relationships, and fan violence inherent to idol fandoms.
